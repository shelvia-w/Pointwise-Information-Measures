Pointwise Information Measures as Confidence
Estimators in Deep Neural Networks:
A Comparative Study

Shelvia Wongso, Rohan Ghosh, Mehul Motani

ICML 2025

This paper provides a comparative study of three pointwise information measures as post-hoc confidence estimators in deep neural networks.

Paper PDF Code Citation

Overview

Reliable confidence estimation is essential for deploying deep neural networks in high-stakes settings. Standard softmax confidence is widely used, but it is often miscalibrated and can be unreliable for failure prediction.

The paper proposes to study three pointwise information (PI) measures as post-hoc confidence estimators: Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI), Pointwise Sliced Mutual Information (PSI), and Pointwise $\mathcal{V}$-Information (PVI). These measures are theoretically grounded in their relevance to predictive uncertainty, with properties such as invariance, convergence rates, and sensitivity to geometric attributes like margin and intrinsic dimensionality.

In this paper, we:

  • study the theoretical properties of the three PI measures, including invariance properties, convergence rates, and geometric characteristics (margin and intrinsic dimensionality), and
  • evaluate the three PI measures for both failure prediction and confidence estimation tasks across a diverse range of datasets and architectures.

Pointwise Information Measures

1. Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI)

PMI measures how strongly a specific feature-label pair \((x,y)\) is associated, relative to what would be expected if \(x\) and \(y\) were independent. It is defined as:

$$ pmi(x;y) := \log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)} $$

2. Pointwise Sliced Mutual Information (PSI)

PSI measures the association between \(x\) and \(y\) through random one-dimensional projections, giving a more scalable approximation in high-dimensional settings. It is defined as:

$$ psi(x;y) := \mathbb{E}_{\substack{\theta\in\Theta, \\ \phi\in\Phi}} \left[ pmi(\theta^T x; \phi^T y) \right] $$

3. Pointwise $\mathcal{V}$-Information (PVI)

PVI measures how much more predictable the label \(y\) becomes from the input \(x\), relative to a null input, under a predictive family \(\mathcal{V}\). It is defined as:

$$ pvi(x \to y) := -\log g[\varnothing](y) + \log g'[x](y) $$

For PVI, we compute it between the input \(x\) and the predicted label \(y\) by training a separate model with the same architecture. This reflects how well the predictive family can infer the label directly from the input. In contrast, PMI and PSI are computed between the model’s output-layer features \(x\) and the predicted label \(y\), since these features more directly capture the network’s confidence.

Results

1. Failure Prediction

The first result evaluates failure prediction, asking whether a confidence score can distinguish correct predictions from errors. Across different models and datasets, PVI consistently achieves the strongest overall performance, with higher AUROC and AUPR and lower FPR95 and E-AURC than the other pointwise information measures and standard confidence baselines.

2. Confidence Calibration

The second result evaluates confidence calibration, asking whether a confidence score matches the true likelihood of correctness. Across different models and datasets, PVI remains competitive and often performs among the strongest methods, showing that its gains in failure prediction do not come at the expense of calibration.
